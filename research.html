Research and Academia

Knee Ligament and Sports Injuries:

  • A clinical device for measuring internal-external rotational laxity of the knee.

    • Co-authors: AMJ Bull, RdW Thomas, AA Amis American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2013;41(1):87-94 Research venue: Departments of Biomechanics & Bioengineering, Imperial College London Mr Alam developed, tested and validated a clinical device for measuring knee laxity. This occurs after knee ligament injury. Injuries to the ACL, MCL, LCL, PLC or PCL or a combination cause knee instability. Injuries cause knee giving way, pain and have a profound effect on patient function. Failure to recognise injuries lead to failure of surgery, and is a leading cause of failed ACL reconstruction. This article presents a novel device which helps clinicians diagnose exact isolated and combined knee injuries. The device continues to be used and is awaiting patent.

    http://ajs.sagepub.com/content/41/1/87.short

  • Measurement of rotational laxity of the knee: in vitro comparison of accuracy between the tibia, overlying skin and the foot.

    • Co-authors: AMJ Bull, RdW Thomas, AA Amis American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2011;29(12):2575-2581 Research venue: Departments of Biomechanics & Bioengineering, Imperial College London This cadaveric study was used to develop a measuring device for measuring knee laxity in sports knee ligament injuries. This study also showed that the clinical diagnosis of multi-ligament using the dial test was more accurate in the supine position compared to the prone position, a critical point particularly in diagnosis and operative planning.

    http://ajs.sagepub.com/content/39/12/2575.abstract

  • Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL) reconstruction with a divergent patellar transverse 2-tunnel technique

    • Co-authors: AS Panni, S Cerciello, M Vasso, N Maffulli American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2011 vol. 39 pp 2647-2655 This study describes a novel method of reconstructing the MPFL, the primary restraint to lateral patella (kneecap) laxity and dislocation. We designed this new method of MPFL reconstruction to replicate the normal anatomy found in human knees. The surgical outcomes were good or very good in the majority of patients.

    http://ajs.sagepub.com/content/39/12/2647

  • Combined Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL) and Posterolateral Corner (PLC) reconstruction in chronic posterolateral instability.

    • Co-authors: C Zorzi, V Iacono, V Madonna, D Rosa, N Maffulli Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology and Arthroscopy, 2013 May;21(5):1036-1042 This article describes a surgical technique for the management of this complex multi-ligament knee injury. The patients were sports persons who had sustained this severe injury and describes the diagnosis, surgical procedure, rehabilitation and outcome in terms of pain, function and return to sports.

    http://link.springer.com/article/10.1007%2Fs00167-011-1771-y

  • Arthroscopic management of chronic patellar tendinopathy (“jumper’s knee”).

    • Co-authors: A Pascarella, F Pascarella, C Latte, M Salvatore, N Maffulli American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2011;39(9):1975-83 Patients with patellar tendinopathy in whom non-operative management is unsuccessful, surgery is an option to return to high levels of physical activity. Although open surgery is traditionally advocated, an arthroscopic approach may be safe and effective. This study shows that key-hole surgery for patients with patellar tendinopathy, unresponsive to non-operative management, appears to provide significant improvements in symptoms and function, with improvements maintained for at least 3 years. These results suggest that some patients may not be able to achieve their pre-symptom sporting level; or if they do, they may participate in sports with some degree of residual symptoms. Early return to sports may also be achieved.

    http://ajs.sagepub.com/content/39/9/1975.long

  • Functional anatomy of the Achilles Tendon.

    • Co-authors: MN Doral, M Bozkurt, E Turhan, OA Atay, G Donmez, N Maffulli Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology and Arthroscopy, 2010;18:638-643 The Achilles tendon is the strongest and thickest tendon in the human body. It is also the commonest tendon to rupture. This article describes the anatomy, vascular supply, nerve innervation and biomechanics of the Achilles tendon. The article serves as a basis for the management of conditions such as Achilles Tendon Ruptures and Achilles Tendinopathy.

    http://link.springer.com/article/10.1007%2Fs00167-010-1083-7

Total Knee Replacement for Knee Arthritis:

  • Changes in knee kinematics reflect the articular geometry after arthroplasty.

    • Co-authors: AMJ Bull, O Kessler, AA Amis Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research, 2008;466(10):2491-9 Research venue: Departments of Biomechanics & Bioengineering, Imperial College London In this study Computer Navigation was used to ensure accurate positioning of Total Knee Replacements which then underwent testing. Changes in the way the artificial knee replacement moved compared to a normal knee were evident at functional knee range of motion, suggesting the differences were due to the design of knee replacements, as opposed to inaccurate positioning of the knee replacement during surgery.

    http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2584306/pdf/11999_2008_Article_440.pdf

  • The effect on knee kinematics of variations in total knee replacement implantation

    • Co-authors: DJ Lucas, O Kessler, AMJ Bull, AA Amis Journal of Biomechanics, 2006;39 Research venue: Departments of Biomechanics & Bioengineering, Imperial College London In this study Computer Navigation was used to ensure accurate positioning of Total Knee Replacements which then underwent testing. Changing the “ideal” or “datum” knee replacement position has significant effects on the way the knee moves. These changes have important effects on movement in a freely moving knee and when moving under normal forces. This has significant implications during surgery and in the management of patients with problems after total knee replacement surgery.

Current Research:

  • Alam Knee Extensor Test (‘AKET’)

    • Mr Alam has a developed a novel simple bedside clinical test which can detect whether the quadriceps tendon, the patellar tendon or the patellar are disrupted. It has been tested and validated in patients in the clinic and the ward, and compared extremely favourably with findings on ultrasound, MRI and during operations. The initial work is being presented at the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS) Annual Meeting in March 2016 in Florida, USA.
